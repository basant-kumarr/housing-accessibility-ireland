# Housing Accessibility in Ireland — Business Consultancy Project

> Real client project for Age Friendly Ireland (AFI), projecting 
> stair-related household accessibility barriers from 2022 to 2050 
> using CSO Census data and population modelling.

---

## Client

**Age Friendly Ireland (AFI)** — works with all Irish local 
authorities to make communities more inclusive and supportive 
for older people. Focus areas include housing, universal design, 
and services that enable people to age with dignity and 
independence.

---

## Business Problem

By 2050, approximately 1 in 5 Irish households may include 
someone who struggles with stairs. AFI needed a defensible, 
data-driven estimate of the percentage of Irish households 
affected by stair-related mobility barriers today and in 
future decades — to guide national housing policy, universal 
design standards, and adaptation planning.

---

## Key Findings

| Year | Households Affected |
|------|-------------------|
| 2022 (baseline) | 16.9% |
| 2030 (projected) | 18.2% |
| 2040 (projected) | 20.2% |
| 2050 (projected) | 22.3% |

- Over **100,000 additional households** will face stair-related 
  barriers by 2050 if policy does not change
- **349,155 people** currently live with mobility difficulties 
  (CSO Census 2022)
- **40.6%** of Irish households already include at least one 
  member with a disability
- Ageing population (65+) is the primary driver — projected to 
  rise from 14% to 26–30% of the population by 2050

---

## Data Sources

- **CSO Census 2022** — Question Q15(c): difficulty with 
  walking, climbing stairs, lifting (F4006 dataset)
- **CSO Population Projections** — PEC19 (M2 scenario)
- **NDA** — National Disability Authority disability context
- International benchmarks: WHO, Eurostat, ABS (Australia), 
  SCB (Sweden), UNECE (Denmark)

---

## Methodology

**Step 1** — Calculate Q15(c) prevalence by sex and age band  
(0–3, 4–64, 65+) from CSO Census 2022

**Step 2** — Apply prevalence rates to CSO population 
projections for 2030, 2040, and 2050

**Step 3** — Convert person-level prevalence into 
household-level percentages using 2022 household-size 
distribution

Why this approach: transparent, reproducible, uses official 
government data, suitable for long-term policy planning.

---

## International Policy Benchmarking

| Country | Policy Model | Key Feature |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Ireland | Minimum standards | Part M/K building regs; means-tested adaptation grants |
| Australia | Prevention-first | Livable Housing Design Standards mandatory (2023) |
| Sweden | Rights-based | Housing Adaptation Act — legal right to adaptations |
| Denmark | Community-driven | Municipal duty to provide accessible housing |

**Gap identified:** Ireland relies on minimum building standards 
and reactive grant schemes — no universal design law, no 
guaranteed right to adaptations, no integrated retrofit policy.

---

## Key Recommendations Delivered to Client

1. Integrate universal design requirements into new building 
   regulations — moving from reactive to preventive
2. Establish a legal right to housing adaptations (modelled on 
   Sweden's Housing Adaptation Act)
3. Integrate accessibility into Ireland's National Retrofit Plan 
   alongside energy efficiency
4. Improve CSO household-level data collection to track 
   caregiving arrangements and housing modification needs
5. Adopt community-driven delivery model (Denmark approach) 
   for local housing adaptation support
